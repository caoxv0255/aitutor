She had learned the recipe from her own mother during the hard winter of 1959, and she treated it as something almost sacred. C

For decades, scientists believed that the adult brain was essentially fixed — that once you passed a certain age, you could not grow new neurons. This view has been overturned by research in the field of neuroplasticity, which demonstrates that the brain retains the ability to reorganize itself throughout life.

Neuroplasticity refers to the brain's capacity to form new neural connections in response to learning, experience, or even injury. When you learn a new language, practice a musical instrument, or recover from a stroke, your brain is physically changing. Synaptic connections strengthen or weaken, and in some regions, new neurons are indeed generated — a process known as neurogenesis.

Research by Dr. Sara Lazar at Harvard Medical School has shown that regular meditation can increase the thickness of the cerebral cortex, particularly in areas associated with attention and emotional regulation. Similarly, studies on London taxi drivers, who must memorize thousands of routes, found that their hippocampi — the brain regions responsible for spatial memory — were significantly larger than those of bus drivers who followed fixed routes.

However, neuroplasticity also has a downside. The same mechanisms that allow the brain to adapt positively can also entrench negative patterns. Chronic stress, anxiety, and unhealthy habits can rewire the brain in harmful ways, making it harder to break free from destructive behaviors. This is why interventions such as cognitive behavioral therapy work: they leverage neuroplasticity to help patients develop healthier thought patterns.

Understanding neuroplasticity has profound implications for education, rehabilitation, and mental health. It suggests that with the right strategies and sustained effort, people of all ages can improve their cognitive abilities and recover from brain injuries — a message that is both scientifically grounded and deeply hopeful.

The rapid expansion of artificial intelligence in education has sparked a fierce debate: should AI be welcomed as a transformative tool or feared as a threat to genuine learning? While proponents argue that AI tutors can personalize instruction and make quality education accessible to all, critics worry that over-reliance on technology will erode critical thinking and diminish the human element of teaching.

There is no denying that AI offers remarkable benefits. Adaptive learning platforms can analyze a student's strengths and weaknesses in real time, tailoring exercises to target specific gaps in knowledge. In remote areas where qualified teachers are scarce, AI-powered tools can bridge the educational divide. During the COVID-19 pandemic, AI-assisted platforms helped millions of students continue learning when schools were closed.

However, education is not merely about transferring information efficiently. The best teachers do more than deliver content — they inspire curiosity, model ethical reasoning, and create environments where students learn to question, debate, and collaborate. These deeply human dimensions of education cannot be replicated by algorithms, no matter how sophisticated. A chatbot can explain the causes of World War I, but it cannot guide a student through the moral complexities of war with the same empathy and wisdom as a skilled teacher.

Moreover, there are legitimate concerns about data privacy, algorithmic bias, and the commercialization of education. When AI systems collect vast amounts of student data to optimize their algorithms, who owns that data? How do we ensure that AI does not reinforce existing inequalities by training predominantly on data from privileged populations?

The most productive approach is not to choose between AI and human teachers, but to integrate both thoughtfully. AI should serve as a tool that frees teachers from repetitive tasks so they can focus on what they do best: mentoring, inspiring, and connecting with students on a human level. The future of education lies not in replacing teachers with machines, but in empowering teachers with technology.
